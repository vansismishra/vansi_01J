History

India's space program began in the 1960s, shortly after the country gained independence. At that time, India was one of the poorest countries in the world, and its leaders recognized that a robust space program could help to address some of the country's most pressing challenges, including food security, communications, and national security.

The Indian Space Research Organization (ISRO) was established in 1969 under the leadership of Vikram Sarabhai, a prominent Indian scientist and industrialist. One of ISRO's first major achievements was the successful launch of the Aryabhata satellite in 1975, which made India the sixth country in the world to launch its own satellite.

Over the next several decades, ISRO continued to develop its capabilities in space technology, focusing on areas such as satellite communications, remote sensing, and meteorology. In 1983, India launched its first satellite for telecommunications, which helped to connect the country's remote areas and improve its communications infrastructure.

In the 1990s, ISRO began to focus on developing its launch vehicle technology, and in 1993 it successfully launched the PSLV (Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle), which has since become one of the most reliable launch vehicles in the world. In 2008, ISRO achieved another major milestone when it successfully launched the Chandrayaan-1 mission, which made India the fourth country to reach the moon.

In recent years, ISRO has continued to expand its capabilities in space technology, with a particular focus on satellite-based navigation and Earth observation. In 2013, ISRO launched the Mangalyaan mission, which made India the first country to successfully reach Mars on its first attempt and at a fraction of the cost of other Mars missions. ISRO has also launched a series of successful remote sensing satellites, which have helped to improve agricultural productivity, disaster management, and national security.

Today, ISRO is recognized as one of the world's leading space agencies, and its achievements have helped to spur economic growth and technological development in India.

Missions

Chandrayaan-1

Chandrayaan-1 was India's first lunar mission, launched on October 22, 2008, by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). The mission was aimed at conducting scientific studies of the Moon, with a particular focus on mapping the Moon's surface, its mineral composition, and the presence of water on the Moon.
The spacecraft was equipped with several scientific instruments, including a high-resolution camera, a spectrometer, and a radar system. These instruments were used to gather data on the Moon's surface and atmosphere, and to create detailed maps of the Moon's topography and mineral composition.
One of the major achievements of the Chandrayaan-1 mission was the discovery of evidence of water molecules on the Moon. This discovery was made by the Moon Mineralogy Mapper (M3) instrument, which detected the presence of water molecules in the polar regions of the Moon.
The mission was originally planned to last for two years, but it was prematurely ended in August 2009, after the spacecraft lost communication with ground control. Despite this setback, the Chandrayaan-1 mission was considered a major success, and it paved the way for India's future space exploration efforts.

Mangalyaan

Mangalyaan, also known as the Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM), was India's first interplanetary mission. It was launched on November 5, 2013, by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) with the objective of studying Mars' atmosphere, climate, geology, and potential for life.
Mangalyaan was designed to orbit Mars and study its atmosphere using scientific instruments such as the Mars Colour Camera (MCC), Mars Exospheric Neutral Composition Analyser (MENCA), and the Thermal Infrared Imaging Spectrometer (TIS). The mission also aimed to study the mineral composition of Mars using a Mars Orbiter Laser Altimeter (MOLA).
The mission was a major success for India, as it made India the first country to successfully reach Mars on its first attempt, and the fourth space agency in the world to reach the Red Planet. The spacecraft entered into orbit around Mars on September 24, 2014, and has been sending back valuable data since then.
The Mangalyaan mission was completed within a budget of approximately $75 million, which is significantly less than the cost of similar missions by other space agencies. This achievement demonstrated India's capability in space exploration and its ability to carry out complex space missions at a relatively low cost.

ASTROSAT

ASTROSAT is India's first multi-wavelength astronomical observatory satellite, launched on September 28, 2015. It was designed to study the universe in various wavelengths of light, from ultraviolet to X-rays.
The ASTROSAT mission aims to study a wide range of astrophysical phenomena, including the behavior of black holes and neutron stars, the evolution of stars and galaxies, and the properties of hot gas in galaxy clusters. The satellite carries five scientific payloads, including Ultraviolet Imaging Telescope (UVIT), Soft X-ray Telescope (SXT), Large Area X-ray Proportional Counter (LAXPC), Cadmium Zinc Telluride Imager (CZTI), and Scanning Sky Monitor (SSM).
The UVIT instrument can observe the universe in the ultraviolet wavelength range, while the SXT and LAXPC instruments can observe the universe in the soft X-ray and hard X-ray wavelength ranges, respectively. The CZTI instrument can detect gamma rays from astrophysical sources, and the SSM instrument can monitor the sky for transient events.
The ASTROSAT mission is a collaborative effort between several institutions, including the Indian Space Research Organization (ISRO), the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research (TIFR), and various other Indian universities and research organizations.

GSAT-19

GSAT-19 is a communication satellite developed by the Indian Space Research Organization (ISRO). It was launched on June 5, 2017, using the Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle Mark III (GSLV Mk III) rocket.
GSAT-19 is considered to be a game-changer in the field of Indian communication satellites, as it has several advanced features that were not present in the previous Indian communication satellites. It has a mass of around 3,136 kg and has been placed in a geostationary orbit at an altitude of around 36,000 km.
The satellite carries several communication transponders in different frequency bands, including Ka-band, Ku-band, and C-band. The Ka-band transponders have a higher data transfer rate compared to the other frequency bands, making it suitable for applications such as high-speed internet and video streaming.
In addition to its communication capabilities, GSAT-19 also carries an experimental instrument called the Geostationary Radiation Spectrometer (GRASP), which is designed to measure the radiation environment in the geostationary orbit.
The successful launch of GSAT-19 has paved the way for India to launch more advanced communication satellites, which would provide better connectivity and internet services to remote areas of the country.

Chandrayaan-2

Chandrayaan-2 is India's second lunar exploration mission, launched on July 22, 2019, by the Indian Space Research Organization (ISRO). The mission was intended to study the lunar surface and the mineral composition of the Moon.
Chandrayaan-2 consisted of an orbiter, a lander, and a rover. The orbiter carried eight scientific instruments to study the lunar surface and the environment around the Moon. The lander, named Vikram, was designed to soft-land on the lunar surface near the south pole region, and the rover, named Pragyan, was designed to move on the lunar surface and conduct experiments.
However, during the final stages of the mission, the lander lost communication with the ground control station and crashed on the lunar surface. Despite this setback, the orbiter continues to function and has provided valuable scientific data about the Moon's surface and environment.
The scientific instruments carried by the orbiter include a high-resolution camera, a spectrometer, a radar, and a payload to detect water on the lunar surface. The mission was aimed at understanding the origin and evolution of the Moon and to explore the possibility of a future manned mission to the Moon.
Chandrayaan-2 was a significant achievement for India's space program, as it demonstrated the country's capability to undertake complex missions to explore the solar system.

Scientists

Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam

Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam (1931-2015) was an Indian scientist, politician, and the 11th President of India (2002-2007). Born in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, India, he was the youngest of five siblings.
Kalam completed his education in Physics and Aerospace Engineering, and later joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) as a scientist. He played a key role in the development of India's first satellite launch vehicle and was known as the "Missile Man of India" for his contributions to the development of ballistic missile technology.
Apart from his scientific achievements, Kalam was also a great writer and a motivational speaker. He authored several books, including "Wings of Fire," an autobiography, and "Ignited Minds," a collection of his speeches and thoughts on the future of India.
In 2002, Kalam was elected as the President of India, becoming the first scientist to hold this position. He was widely respected for his humility, integrity, and dedication to public service. He continued to inspire people through his speeches and lectures even after his term as President ended.
Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam passed away on July 27, 2015, while delivering a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management Shillong. He left behind a legacy of scientific achievements and a vision for a prosperous and peaceful India.


Dr. Vikram Sarabhai

Dr. Vikram Sarabhai (1919-1971) was an Indian physicist and industrialist, widely regarded as the father of India's space program. Born in Ahmedabad, India, Sarabhai completed his education in physics from the University of Cambridge, UK.
Upon his return to India, Sarabhai founded the Physical Research Laboratory in Ahmedabad, which is now one of the premier research institutions in the country. He also played a key role in the establishment of the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and was the chairman of the organization from 1963 until his death.
Under Sarabhai's leadership, ISRO launched its first satellite, Aryabhata, in 1975, and went on to achieve several other milestones in the field of space technology. He was also instrumental in the establishment of several other institutions, including the Ahmedabad Textile Industry's Research Association and the Indian Institute of Management Ahmedabad.
Apart from his scientific achievements, Sarabhai was also a philanthropist and a champion of social causes. He was a strong advocate for the development of rural India and believed that science and technology could play a key role in addressing the country's socio-economic challenges.
Dr. Vikram Sarabhai passed away in 1971, at the young age of 52, leaving behind a legacy of scientific and social contributions that continue to inspire generations of Indians.


Dr. Satish Dhawan

Dr. Satish Dhawan (1920-2002) was an Indian aerospace engineer and mathematician who played a key role in the development of India's space program. Born in Srinagar, India, Dhawan completed his education in physics and mathematics from Lahore and Cambridge universities, respectively.
After returning to India, Dhawan worked at the Indian Institute of Science in Bangalore and later joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) in 1972. He served as the chairman of ISRO from 1972 to 1984 and was instrumental in the development of several satellite launch vehicles, including the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV), which has become the workhorse of India's space program.
Dhawan was also a strong advocate for international cooperation in space research and played a key role in establishing ties between ISRO and several other space agencies, including NASA and the European Space Agency.
Apart from his contributions to space technology, Dhawan was also a renowned mathematician and a respected academician. He was the founder-director of the Indian Institute of Technology, Kanpur, and served as the vice-chancellor of the University of Delhi.
Dr. Satish Dhawan passed away in 2002, leaving behind a legacy of scientific and academic contributions that continue to inspire generations of Indians.


Tessy Thomas

Tessy Thomas (also known as "Missile Woman of India") is an Indian scientist and engineer who played a key role in the development of India's ballistic missile technology. Born in Kerala, India, Thomas completed her education in engineering from the Government Engineering College, Thrissur, and later joined the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) as a scientist.
Thomas rose through the ranks at DRDO and played a key role in the development of several missiles, including the Agni and Prithvi series of ballistic missiles. She served as the project director for the Agni IV and V missile systems and is credited with developing the guidance and control systems for these missiles.
In 2012, Thomas was appointed as the director of the Advanced Systems Laboratory, which is responsible for the development of missile systems in India. She became the first woman to hold this position in DRDO's history.
Apart from her contributions to missile technology, Thomas is also a strong advocate for gender equality and women's empowerment. She has spoken publicly about the need to encourage more women to pursue careers in science and technology and has served as a role model for young women in India.
For her contributions to science and technology, Thomas has received several awards and honours, including the Lal Bahadur Shastri National Award for Excellence in Public Administration, Academics and Management in 2012, and the Padma Shri, one of India's highest civilian awards, in 2013.


U. R. Rao

Udupi Ramachandra Rao (1932-2017), popularly known as U. R. Rao, was an Indian space scientist and former chairman of the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). Born in Karnataka, India, Rao completed his education in engineering and earned a doctorate in cosmic rays from the Physical Research Laboratory in Ahmedabad, India.
Rao played a key role in the development of India's space program and was one of the driving forces behind the establishment of ISRO's satellite program. He served as the chairman of ISRO from 1984 to 1994 and oversaw the successful launch of several satellites, including the INSAT and IRS series.
Apart from his contributions to space technology, Rao was also a respected academician and served as a professor of space technology at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and as the chancellor of the Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore. He authored several books on space science and technology and was a member of several international scientific bodies.
Rao received numerous awards and honours for his contributions to science and technology, including the Padma Bhushan and Padma Vibhushan, two of India's highest civilian awards. He was also a Fellow of the Royal Society, London, and the International Academy of Astronautics.
U. R. Rao passed away in 2017, leaving behind a legacy of scientific and academic contributions that continue to inspire generations of Indians.


Mylswamy Annadurai

Mylswamy Annadurai is an Indian scientist and engineer who played a key role in the Indian Space Research Organisation's (ISRO) Chandrayaan-1 and Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM) missions. Born in Tamil Nadu, India, Annadurai completed his education in engineering from the PSG College of Technology in Coimbatore, India.
Annadurai joined ISRO in 1982 and worked on several satellite and remote sensing projects before being appointed as the project director for the Chandrayaan-1 mission in 2003. Chandrayaan-1 was India's first lunar mission, and Annadurai played a key role in its successful launch and operation.
Following the success of Chandrayaan-1, Annadurai was appointed as the project director for the Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM), which was launched in 2013. MOM was India's first interplanetary mission and made India the first country to successfully put a satellite in Mars orbit on its first attempt. Annadurai's leadership and technical expertise were instrumental in the success of both missions.
Apart from his contributions to space technology, Annadurai is also a respected academician and has authored several books on space science and technology. He has received numerous awards and honours for his contributions, including the Padma Shri, one of India's highest civilian awards, in 2016.
Annadurai's dedication and achievements have made him a role model for young scientists and engineers in India, inspiring them to pursue careers in science and technology.


K. Rahakrishnan

K Radhakrishnan is an Indian space scientist who has contributed significantly to India's space program. Born in Kerala, India, Radhakrishnan completed his education in engineering from the Government Engineering College, Thrissur, and later earned a doctorate in aerospace engineering from the Indian Institute of Technology, Delhi.
Radhakrishnan joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) in 1971 and played a key role in the development of several satellite and launch vehicle programs. He served as the project director for the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV) program, which has become one of the most reliable and successful launch vehicles in the world.
Radhakrishnan was appointed as the chairman of ISRO in 2009 and served in this position until 2014. During his tenure, he oversaw several important missions, including the successful Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM) and the development of the Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle (GSLV) program.
Apart from his contributions to space technology, Radhakrishnan is also a respected academician and has authored several books on space science and technology. He has received numerous awards and honours for his contributions, including the Padma Bhushan and Padma Vibhushan, two of India's highest civilian awards.
Radhakrishnan's dedication and leadership have made him a key figure in India's space program, and his contributions continue to inspire young scientists and engineers in India and around the world.


R. Chidambaram

R. Chidambaram (Rajagopala Chidambaram) is an Indian physicist and scientific advisor to the Indian government. He was born on November 12, 1936, in Chennai, India. He obtained his Bachelor's degree in Science from Loyola College in Chennai, and his Master's degree in Science from Presidency College, Chennai. He later earned a Ph.D. in Physics from the University of Madras in 1962.
Chidambaram has made significant contributions to the field of condensed matter physics, specifically in the areas of neutron scattering and high-pressure physics. He has also served as the Director of the Bhabha Atomic Research Centre (BARC) in Mumbai and as the Chairman of the Atomic Energy Commission of India.
In addition to his scientific work, Chidambaram has also played an important role in shaping science and technology policies in India. He has served as the Principal Scientific Adviser to the Government of India and as the Chairman of the Scientific Advisory Committee to the Cabinet. He has received numerous awards and honors for his contributions to science, including the Padma Vibhushan, the second-highest civilian award in India, in 2000.


G. Madhavan Nair

G. Madhavan Nair (Gopalasamudram Madhavan Nair) is an Indian space scientist and former Chairman of the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). He was born on October 31, 1943, in Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, India.
Nair received his Bachelor's degree in Engineering from College of Engineering, Trivandrum and his Master's degree in Engineering from Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore. He joined ISRO in 1967 and has made significant contributions to the Indian space program.
During his tenure as Chairman of ISRO from 2003 to 2009, Nair oversaw several important missions, including the successful launch of Chandrayaan-1, India's first lunar mission. He also played a key role in the development of India's Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle (GSLV) and Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV).
In addition to his work at ISRO, Nair has also served as the Secretary of the Department of Space and as the Chairman of the Space Commission of India. He has received numerous awards and honors for his contributions to science and technology, including the Padma Vibhushan, the second-highest civilian award in India, in 2009.


Kalpana Chawla

Kalpana Chawla was an Indian-American astronaut who was the first woman of Indian origin to go to space. She was born on March 17, 1962, in Karnal, Haryana, India, and died on February 1, 2003, in the Space Shuttle Columbia disaster.
Chawla obtained a Bachelor's degree in Aeronautical Engineering from Punjab Engineering College in India and later moved to the United States to pursue higher education. She obtained a Master's degree in Aerospace Engineering from the University of Texas at Arlington and a Ph.D. in Aerospace Engineering from the University of Colorado Boulder.
Chawla was selected as a NASA astronaut candidate in 1994 and flew her first space mission as a mission specialist on the Space Shuttle Columbia in 1997. She went on to fly a second mission on the same spacecraft in 2003.
Chawla is remembered for her contributions to space exploration and for breaking barriers for women and people of Indian origin in the field of space. She received several awards and honors, including the Congressional Space Medal of Honor and the NASA Space Flight Medal.


Rakesh Shrama

Rakesh Sharma is a retired Indian Air Force officer and astronaut who became the first Indian to travel to space. He was born on January 13, 1949, in Punjab, India.
Sharma joined the Indian Air Force in 1970 and became a test pilot. In 1982, he was selected as a cosmonaut to participate in the joint space mission between India and the Soviet Union called the Soyuz T-11. He flew into space aboard the Soyuz T-11 spacecraft on April 2, 1984, spending nearly eight days in space as part of the international crew.
During his space mission, Sharma conducted scientific experiments and observations on behalf of the Indian government. He also spoke with then-Prime Minister of India, Indira Gandhi, over radio communication, famously stating, "Saare jahan se achcha, Hindustan hamara" (Our India is the best of all).
After his space mission, Sharma returned to the Indian Air Force and later retired as a Wing Commander. He was awarded several honors, including the Ashoka Chakra, India's highest peacetime gallantry award.


Sunita Williams

Sunita Williams is an Indian-American astronaut who has spent a total of 322 days in space on two separate space missions. She was born on September 19, 1965, in Euclid, Ohio, in the United States.
Williams obtained a Bachelor's degree in Physical Science from the United States Naval Academy and a Master's degree in Engineering Management from Florida Institute of Technology. She was commissioned as an officer in the United States Navy in 1987 and later became a test pilot.
In 1998, Williams was selected as a NASA astronaut candidate and went on to fly her first space mission in 2006 as a flight engineer on board the Space Shuttle Discovery. She spent a total of 195 days in space, setting the record for the longest spaceflight by a woman at that time.
Williams went on to fly her second space mission in 2012 as a member of the Expedition 32/33 crew on the International Space Station (ISS). She spent a total of 127 days in space during that mission, conducting scientific experiments and performing spacewalks.
Williams is a veteran of two spaceflights and has spent a total of 322 days in space. She has received several awards and honors for her contributions to space exploration, including the NASA Spaceflight Medal, the Navy Commendation Medal, and the Navy and Marine Corps Achievement Medal.
